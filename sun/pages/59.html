Essays, Memoirs, and True Stories

Good Enough

  • By Beth Mayer
  • July 2006

What can I trust my mother to do? She will usually come when I need her. She will love my children as fiercely as I do, but in an older, less-complicated way. She will frequently enrage me.

Cry In The Wilderness

  • By Carroll Ann Susco
  • July 2006

Unless I tell people about the voices, they don’t know. I’m not sure how this can be: that they don’t hear them, too. It’s suspicious, in fact. I want to crawl inside their heads and listen, see for myself where their thoughts come from.

You’re In Here, Too

  • By Jim Ralston
  • July 2006

It’s morning but still dark out. It’s also raining and cold. I’m walking out of the twenty-four-hour fitness center, on my way to the all-night Waffle House, when a woman hails me from her car. She has just run away from her husband, she says, and needs gas money to get to her mother’s.

Sweet Rolls And Vodka

  • By Victoria Patterson
  • July 2006

At sunrise you climbed through your bedroom window at the recovery home and found a note waiting on your untouched pillow: “This was your final warning. Pack today.”

Island Of The Damned

  • By Jack Hitt
  • July 2006

Oh, and there is one other problem — the elephant sitting in the room, and certainly the most profound explanation for Nauru’s contemporary interest in money laundering: a century of phosphate mining has denuded roughly 80 percent of the island.

Where The Water Is

  • By Jan Shoemaker
  • June 2006

One of the uncomfortable things about living with a person who suffers from Alzheimer’s is that it makes you confront your own character flaws.

Men Who Make Houses

  • By Linda McCullough Moore
  • June 2006

I know the life story of every man who has so much as hammered in a nail at my house. This is not my doing. I’m disinclined to delve too deeply into the depths of anybody else’s dire experience. I’ve got my hands full with my own.

Red Politics And Blue In Wyoming

  • By David Romtvedt
  • June 2006

I’ve spent many years repairing windmills with my father-in-law at his Four Mile Ranch. The mills pump water to the surface for cattle and sheep to drink. There are nineteen of these windmills on this broken patch of land, which looks west to the Bighorn Mountains and east to Powder River.

Diagnostic Tools

  • By Robin Cunningham
  • June 2006

They breathe deeply and put their hot hands on me. I lie on their padded massage table, on their soft sheets. Light slants across the room and into the kitchen, illuminating the place where my seventeen-year-old dog lies on his bed, unable to move without human hands to hoist his hind end up, a human voice to encourage him on his journey into the leaf-covered yard.

Waiting For Salmon

  • By Barry Lopez
  • June 2006

How global warming will affect the fate of chinook salmon, and all that’s tied to them, is one of the many Gordian knots in natural history blithely dismissed by Americans still trying to pull Charles Darwin’s pants down.
